Biodiversity Knowledge

Speciedex

Speciedex is an open biodiversity, taxonomy, conservation, research, and species-information platform designed to help people discover, compare, study, document, and understand life on Earth.

The project brings together scientific classification, species descriptions, geographic ranges, conservation status, habitats, media, research references, observation records, specimen data, ecological relationships, and educational resources within a unified public system.

Speciedex is built for researchers, students, educators, conservationists, naturalists, software developers, data engineers, institutions, citizen scientists, and anyone curious about the living world.

A Living Index of Life

Speciedex is designed as a continuously evolving index of biological diversity.

Each species record can connect scientific names, accepted taxonomy, synonyms, common names, classification, morphology, behavior, ecology, habitat, range, conservation status, media, references, observations, specimens, genetic data, and related species.

The goal is not merely to display isolated facts, but to create a navigable structure through which users can understand how organisms relate to one another and to the environments they inhabit.

More Than a Species List

Biodiversity cannot be understood through names alone.

A species exists within ecosystems, food webs, geographic regions, evolutionary lineages, conservation pressures, climatic conditions, and relationships with other organisms.

Speciedex is designed to connect these dimensions so that a user can move naturally from one species to its genus, family, habitat, region, conservation status, ecological role, associated media, and supporting research.

Project Mission

Make Biodiversity Knowledge Easier to Explore

Speciedex exists to improve access to biological, ecological, geographic, and conservation information.

Scientific knowledge is distributed across databases, journals, museum catalogues, government records, university repositories, field reports, archives, documentary media, citizen-science networks, and specialist publications.

The project seeks to organize this fragmented information into a coherent public interface while preserving attribution, source context, scientific uncertainty, taxonomic history, and the distinction between primary evidence and secondary interpretation.

Speciedex is intended to support discovery, education, research, conservation planning, software development, independent verification, and long-term preservation of biodiversity knowledge.

Classification

Taxonomy and Scientific Names

Accepted Names

Each species record can identify the currently accepted scientific name and the authority associated with its formal description.

Accepted names may change as new evidence, genetic analysis, revised morphology, nomenclatural decisions, or taxonomic review alters scientific understanding.

Synonyms

Historical names, alternate combinations, misspellings, invalid names, junior synonyms, and obsolete classifications can remain valuable for finding older literature and museum records.

Speciedex is designed to preserve these relationships rather than silently replacing every prior name.

Taxonomic Hierarchy

Species can be organized within genera, families, orders, classes, phyla, kingdoms, domains, clades, and other recognized biological groupings.

The platform can also support ranks below species, including subspecies, varieties, forms, strains, populations, and domesticated lineages where scientifically appropriate.

Competing Classifications

Different authorities may disagree about the correct placement, rank, boundaries, or validity of a taxon.

Speciedex is intended to preserve source attribution and make disagreement visible rather than presenting all taxonomic questions as permanently settled.

Species Pages

Structured Species Records

A Speciedex species page is intended to function as a structured research and discovery record.

Depending upon available data, a record may include the scientific name, authority, common names, taxonomic hierarchy, diagnostic description, morphology, life history, diet, reproduction, behavior, habitat, geographic distribution, seasonal movement, population status, conservation assessment, legal protection, threats, ecological role, known predators, prey, symbiotic relationships, parasites, diseases, specimen records, genetic identifiers, media, references, and observation history.

Records may also include notes concerning uncertainty, conflicting sources, incomplete evidence, undescribed populations, cryptic species, hybridization, recent taxonomic changes, and historical misidentification.

Relationships

Ecology and Species Interactions

Species do not exist independently of one another.

Predation, herbivory, pollination, seed dispersal, parasitism, mutualism, competition, decomposition, disease transmission, habitat engineering, and nutrient cycling connect organisms into complex ecological networks.

Speciedex is designed to represent these relationships so that users can explore not only what an organism is, but what it eats, what eats it, what it pollinates, what it disperses, what depends upon it, what threatens it, and how its presence or absence changes an ecosystem.

These connections may be represented through linked species records, ecological role labels, habitat pages, food-web models, interaction tables, scientific references, and regional ecosystem summaries.

Place-Based Discovery

Explore Species by Geography

Speciedex is designed to support browsing by country, territory, island, ocean, sea, river basin, mountain range, protected area, ecoregion, biome, climate zone, elevation, latitude, and other geographic divisions.

A user may begin with a place and discover the species recorded there, or begin with a species and explore its known range.

Geographic records can distinguish native range, introduced range, invasive presence, migration, seasonal occurrence, breeding range, wintering range, historical range, uncertain occurrence, and local extinction.

Range information should be interpreted carefully because records may be incomplete, outdated, spatially imprecise, restricted for conservation reasons, or influenced by uneven observation effort.

Protection and Recovery

Conservation Information

Conservation Status

Records may include global, regional, national, and local conservation assessments from recognized authorities.

A species may have different statuses under different assessment systems or across different parts of its range.

Population Trends

Where available, records may identify whether a population is increasing, decreasing, stable, fragmented, recovering, or insufficiently known.

Threats

Habitat loss, hunting, trade, pollution, disease, invasive species, climate change, bycatch, conflict, infrastructure, disturbance, and other pressures can be linked to affected taxa.

Protection

Records may reference protected areas, recovery plans, breeding programs, habitat restoration, trade restrictions, legal protections, and conservation initiatives.

Sensitive Data

Precise location information may be withheld, generalized, delayed, or restricted when publication could increase poaching, collecting pressure, disturbance, persecution, or habitat damage.

Conservation Context

A status label alone cannot describe the full condition of a species.

Assessment date, geographic scope, criteria, confidence, population structure, and data quality must also be considered.

Scientific Sources

Data Integration

Speciedex may integrate or reference information from recognized taxonomic databases, conservation authorities, natural-history museums, herbaria, specimen repositories, scientific publications, open-data systems, citizen-science platforms, genetic databases, government agencies, mapping systems, and research institutions.

Imported or referenced records should preserve source identifiers, attribution, license information, retrieval dates, original naming, and relevant scientific context.

Data from different sources may conflict. One source may recognize a species that another treats as a subspecies or synonym. Geographic records may differ in precision. Conservation assessments may have different publication dates or scopes.

The platform is intended to make these differences visible and traceable rather than flattening every source into an unexplained single answer.

Scientific Reliability

Data Quality and Uncertainty

Biodiversity data is rarely complete.

Some regions are well documented while others remain poorly surveyed. Some species are studied for centuries, while others are known from a single specimen, photograph, sound recording, environmental sample, or historical report.

Speciedex records may include provisional names, uncertain identifications, disputed classifications, unresolved synonyms, outdated range information, incomplete conservation assessments, and source records that require review.

The platform should distinguish verified information from inference, machine-generated suggestions, community submissions, and unresolved claims.

Corrections, supporting evidence, and transparent revision history are essential parts of maintaining a trustworthy biological knowledge system.

Computational Biology

Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

Artificial intelligence may assist with species identification, image classification, acoustic recognition, duplicate detection, metadata extraction, literature review, taxonomic reconciliation, translation, mapping, anomaly detection, and data-quality assessment.

Machine-generated results should not be treated as final scientific authority without validation.

Similar-looking species, cryptic taxa, regional variation, incomplete training data, poor images, mislabeled reference material, and environmental noise can produce incorrect classifications.

Speciedex is intended to present AI-assisted output with appropriate confidence, provenance, review status, and human oversight.

Public Participation

Citizen Science

Public observations can document species distributions, migration, seasonal activity, breeding, rare sightings, invasive expansion, unusual behavior, disease, and local ecological change.

High-quality citizen-science records may include a date, location, photograph, audio recording, video, habitat description, identification notes, observer information, and supporting evidence.

Community observations should be reviewed according to evidence quality, identification difficulty, geographic plausibility, source reputation, and conservation sensitivity.

Speciedex recognizes that local naturalists, photographers, fishers, farmers, hikers, divers, birders, students, guides, and Indigenous knowledge holders often possess detailed knowledge unavailable in formal databases.

Responsible Use

Privacy, Ethics, and Sensitive Biodiversity Data

Biodiversity information can be beneficial for science, conservation, education, and public awareness, but it can also be misused.

Precise locations of rare plants, nesting sites, endangered animals, breeding colonies, roosts, dens, caves, sensitive reefs, and commercially valuable species may increase risks from poaching, collecting, harassment, trafficking, habitat disturbance, or exploitation.

Speciedex may obscure, generalize, delay, restrict, or decline to publish sensitive information when public disclosure presents a credible conservation or safety risk.

The platform should also respect Indigenous knowledge, community rights, cultural restrictions, private land, researcher embargoes, specimen ownership, source terms, and applicable data-protection law.

Scientific Accuracy Notice

Speciedex is an informational, educational, research, and software project.

Biological classification, conservation status, geographic range, scientific interpretation, and species identification can change as new evidence becomes available.

Information may be incomplete, outdated, disputed, provisional, machine-assisted, or subject to source-specific limitations.
