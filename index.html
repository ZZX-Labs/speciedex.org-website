Open Biodiversity Infrastructure

What is Speciedex?

Speciedex is an open-source, Bitcoin-native biodiversity infrastructure project designed to catalogue, organize, reference, analyze, distribute, and preserve information relating to every known species on Earth. It combines modern taxonomy, scientific metadata, biological records, distributed systems, archival preservation, software tools, open documentation, and public educational resources within a unified digital ecosystem.

Speciedex is intended to serve researchers, taxonomists, conservationists, universities, museums, biological repositories, environmental organizations, software developers, educators, students, citizen scientists, and the general public. Its purpose is not merely to display species records, but to establish durable digital infrastructure for studying and preserving the biological knowledge upon which scientific research and conservation depend.

The complete Speciedex ecosystem contains ten coordinated parts. Speciedex is the primary project, public species index, and organizing platform. It is supported by nine specialized components: SpeciedexCore, SpeciedexExplorer, SpeciedexNet, SpeciedexWeb, SpeciedexTerminal, SpeciedexArchives, SpeciedexGeneticBank, SpeciedexApp, and SpeciedexAPI.

Together, these ten parts provide database infrastructure, normalization and validation, web and terminal interfaces, network synchronization, permanent archives, genetic-data references, mobile and desktop access, machine-readable APIs, and public tools for exploring the relationships between organisms, classifications, habitats, genomes, scientific literature, and conservation records.

Ten Integrated Platforms

The Speciedex Ecosystem

Each part of Speciedex is designed to operate independently where necessary while remaining interoperable with the wider platform. Shared identifiers, schemas, metadata policies, archival formats, verification procedures, and synchronization protocols allow data to move consistently between the public website, local software, distributed nodes, research databases, archival repositories, and third-party applications.

One Dataset, Many Interfaces

Shared Infrastructure

Every Speciedex component operates upon a common foundation of normalized records, persistent identifiers, provider provenance, validation rules, versioned schemas, licensing metadata, cryptographic checksums, and reproducible data processing. This common foundation prevents the website, application, API, terminal tools, distributed nodes, and archives from becoming incompatible copies of the same information.

Canonical Taxonomy

Consistent taxonomic ranks, names, authorities, synonyms, lineage relationships, accepted statuses, and conflict-resolution policies.

Provider Provenance

Every imported value can retain its source provider, retrieval date, license, original identifier, transformation history, and verification status.

Open Schemas

Public JSON schemas, examples, policies, tests, documentation, and validation tools make Speciedex data understandable and independently reproducible.

Versioned Archives

Historical snapshots preserve how taxonomic records, classifications, provider data, and project policies change over time.

Distributed Verification

Signed manifests, content hashes, mirror verification, and reproducible builds support integrity across independent SpeciedexNet operators.

Multiple Export Formats

Structured exports may be provided in JSON, JSONL, NDJSON, CSV, SQLite, MariaDB-compatible SQL, text, document, archive, and publication formats.

Open Scientific Utility

Research, Education, and Public Use

Speciedex is designed to support both professional scientific work and public learning. A taxonomist may use SpeciedexTerminal and SpeciedexAPI to compare provider records, while a student may use SpeciedexWeb or SpeciedexApp to explore the same species through maps, classifications, illustrations, and educational summaries. A museum may operate a SpeciedexNet mirror, while an archivist may preserve provider snapshots through SpeciedexArchives.

The project therefore treats public accessibility, reproducibility, technical interoperability, scientific citation, data provenance, and long-term preservation as parts of the same problem. Biological knowledge is most useful when it can be inspected, verified, preserved, cited, exported, analyzed, and reused without dependence upon a proprietary interface or inaccessible data silo.

Scientific Research

Query normalized taxonomic records, compare authorities, trace revisions, analyze provider conflicts, and export reproducible datasets.

Conservation

Connect species identities with conservation status, geographic ranges, ecological records, threats, protected areas, and cited sources.

Education

Provide students and educators with accessible species pages, taxonomic trees, visualizations, references, media, and downloadable learning materials.

Software Development

Build applications, visualizations, datasets, research tools, educational software, and institutional services upon documented open interfaces.

Institutional Archives

Preserve complete provider snapshots, documentation, schemas, licensing records, reports, and historical taxonomy in independently verifiable archives.

Citizen Science

Enable the public to learn about organisms, maintain personal collections, inspect scientific sources, and contribute carefully governed observations.

Open Monetary and Verification Layer

Bitcoin-Native Infrastructure

Speciedex is Bitcoin-native because it is designed around open protocols, independent verification, durable identifiers, distributed operation, transparent funding, and resistance to centralized control. Bitcoin may support donations, institutional funding, contributor payments, node operation, archival sponsorship, public grants, and other project services without requiring Speciedex to depend entirely upon conventional payment processors.

Where technically appropriate, Speciedex may also use Bitcoin block data, cryptographic commitments, timestamping techniques, signed manifests, and independently verifiable hashes to strengthen the provenance and integrity of published datasets. These systems are intended to supplement ordinary scientific citation and archival practice rather than replace them.

Inspect, Verify, Extend

Open-Source Development

Speciedex is developed as open infrastructure. Source code, schemas, tests, provider documentation, policies, examples, reports, command-line tools, archive utilities, and public interfaces are intended to be inspectable and independently verifiable. Development decisions should remain documented so that researchers and contributors can understand not only what the system produces, but how and why it produces it.
