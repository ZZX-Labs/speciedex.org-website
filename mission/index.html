Our Mission

Preserve and Open the Knowledge of Life on Earth

Speciedex exists to organize, verify, preserve, and freely distribute biodiversity knowledge through open science, open software, transparent standards, and resilient public infrastructure.

Mission Statement

The mission of Speciedex is to build and maintain a permanent, open, interoperable, and independently verifiable digital index of Earth's biodiversity.

Speciedex brings together taxonomy, scientific literature, geographic distribution, conservation information, ecological relationships, genetic references, media, specimen records, and historical classifications within a transparent public framework.

The project is designed to make biodiversity knowledge easier to discover, verify, preserve, exchange, and reuse while respecting scientific attribution, licensing, privacy, conservation risk, and cultural sensitivity.

The Problem

Biodiversity knowledge is vast, essential, and dangerously fragmented.

Scientific knowledge about species is distributed across universities, museums, journals, field stations, government agencies, conservation organizations, independent databases, specimen collections, archives, private research systems, and historical publications.

These resources often use different identifiers, taxonomic systems, formats, interfaces, licensing terms, and technical standards. Important information may be duplicated, isolated, difficult to search, inaccessible to the public, or dependent upon aging software and institutions.

Scientific names change. Classifications are revised. Datasets disappear. Web services close. Publications become inaccessible. Media loses attribution. Geographic records become detached from their original sources. The result is a global body of knowledge that is valuable but difficult to preserve and connect.

Fragmented Information

Species records are distributed across incompatible databases, publications, archives, and institutional systems.

Unstable References

Links, identifiers, databases, and software platforms may disappear or change without preserving historical context.

Unequal Access

Researchers, educators, communities, and the public do not have equal access to scientific literature and biodiversity data.

Loss of Attribution

Scientific sources, creators, collectors, institutions, licenses, and revision histories are frequently separated from reused data.

Preservation Risk

Valuable records may depend upon a single institution, server, provider, funding program, or proprietary format.

Limited Interoperability

Inconsistent schemas and identifiers make it difficult to compare, merge, validate, and reuse information across systems.

Our Response

Build shared infrastructure rather than another isolated database.

Speciedex is not intended to replace museums, universities, journals, government databases, conservation systems, taxonomic authorities, or existing biodiversity networks.

The project instead provides open infrastructure capable of connecting these resources through stable identifiers, documented schemas, transparent provenance, public software, interoperable APIs, reproducible datasets, and independently verifiable releases.

Index

Verify

Mission Objectives

01

Document Known Biodiversity

Build structured records for known species, extinct species, fossil taxa, microorganisms, hybrids, subspecies, varieties, forms, disputed taxa, historical classifications, and newly described life.

02

Preserve Scientific Context

Maintain the literature, authorities, specimen records, taxonomic history, evidence, attribution, uncertainty, and revisions associated with each record.

03

Provide Stable Public References

Create durable identifiers and versioned records that remain usable as names, classifications, standards, and software systems change.

04

Enable Independent Verification

Publish source references, manifests, checksums, signatures, timestamps, release metadata, and revision histories that can be independently reviewed.

05

Support Open Scientific Exchange

Provide documented APIs, schemas, exports, software libraries, and reusable tools that allow institutions and developers to exchange information.

06

Build Resilient Infrastructure

Support independent mirrors, archival nodes, offline copies, reproducible deployments, distributed storage, and recovery from institutional or technical failure.

07

Protect Sensitive Biodiversity

Reduce unnecessary exposure of exact locations, protected habitats, rare populations, culturally sensitive knowledge, and data that could facilitate exploitation.

08

Expand Global Access

Improve multilingual, accessible, educational, low-bandwidth, and offline access to biodiversity information across regions and communities.

Our Commitments

Open Software

Core software should remain inspectable, auditable, reproducible, and available under permissive open-source licensing.

Open Standards

Public schemas, stable identifiers, documented interfaces, and portable formats should support broad interoperability.

Scientific Transparency

Records should distinguish accepted information, provisional claims, disputed classifications, historical names, and unresolved uncertainty.

Source Attribution

Original authors, researchers, institutions, collectors, publishers, creators, licenses, and data sources should remain connected to reused information.

Public Accountability

Material technical, scientific, and governance decisions should be documented and open to review whenever practical.

Responsible Publication

Open access must be balanced against conservation risk, privacy, legal restrictions, research ethics, and cultural responsibility.

Long-Term Availability

Public datasets, software releases, documentation, signatures, and historical records should be preserved beyond individual services and release cycles.

Independent Operation

Qualified organizations and individuals should be able to operate mirrors, archives, software deployments, and verification systems independently.

Bitcoin-Native Principles

Applying open verification and distributed resilience to scientific infrastructure.

Bitcoin demonstrated that globally important infrastructure can be built through public protocols, independently verifiable records, open-source software, cryptographic integrity, distributed participation, and transparent rules.

Speciedex adopts these principles where they strengthen biodiversity preservation. This does not mean placing every biological record directly on the Bitcoin blockchain. It means designing systems that can be independently operated, inspected, verified, reproduced, archived, and restored.

Verification Over Trust

Users should be able to verify datasets, software releases, archives, and records rather than relying exclusively upon institutional claims.

Public Protocols

Open interfaces and specifications allow independent implementations and reduce dependence upon a single vendor or organization.

Distributed Availability

Mirrors, archives, local deployments, and independent operators improve resilience against technical, financial, and political failure.

Cryptographic Integrity

Digital signatures, checksums, manifests, and timestamped references help establish authenticity and detect modification.

What Success Looks Like

Success is not defined only by the number of species records stored in the system. A large database without provenance, accessibility, verification, resilience, or scientific context would not fulfill the mission.

Coverage

Broad representation of known life across kingdoms, regions, habitats, taxonomic ranks, and historical periods.

Quality

Records supported by traceable sources, transparent validation states, expert review, and documented uncertainty.

Accessibility

Public browsing, exports, APIs, multilingual interfaces, educational tools, and low-bandwidth or offline access.

Interoperability

Stable identifiers, documented schemas, compatible formats, and meaningful links to external scientific resources.

Resilience

Independent mirrors, archives, reproducible deployments, portable datasets, and recovery procedures.

Participation

Meaningful contributions from researchers, institutions, developers, educators, communities, and volunteers worldwide.

What Speciedex Is Not

Not a Replacement for Scientific Authority

Speciedex records and connects scientific claims but does not replace peer review, taxonomic expertise, field research, museums, journals, or recognized authorities.

Not a Single Unchanging Taxonomy

Biological classification evolves. The project should preserve competing systems, uncertainty, historical context, and revisions rather than conceal disagreement.

Not an Unrestricted Location Directory

The project will not publish sensitive locations without considering conservation threats, legal restrictions, research ethics, and community interests.

Not Dependent on One Institution

The architecture should permit preservation and continued operation even when individual organizations, services, or maintainers are unavailable.
