Instant Bitcoin Payments

Lightning Network

The Lightning Network is a decentralized payment-channel network built upon Bitcoin.

It allows participants to send and receive bitcoin rapidly without recording every individual payment directly in the Bitcoin blockchain.

Lightning extends Bitcoin through cryptographically secured payment channels, routed payments, smart contracts, liquidity management, and final settlement through the base network.

What Lightning Is

Lightning is a network of Bitcoin payment channels.

Participants commit bitcoin to on-chain contracts and then exchange updated balances without publishing every payment to the blockchain.

Payments may travel through several connected nodes, allowing users to transact even when they do not share a direct channel.

Why Lightning Exists

Bitcoin's base layer prioritizes security, decentralization, independent verification, and durable settlement.

Lightning allows frequent and low-value payments to occur outside the blockchain while preserving the ability to settle channel balances through Bitcoin.

This layered approach supports faster payments, reduced on-chain transaction demand, and new payment-based applications.

Network Characteristics

Core Properties

Fast

Lightning payments can settle between participants without waiting for a new Bitcoin block.

Low Cost

Routing fees are often substantially lower than the cost of publishing a separate on-chain transaction.

Bitcoin Native

Lightning uses bitcoin as its underlying asset and settles through the Bitcoin blockchain.

Decentralized

No central operator controls the Lightning Network or determines which compatible nodes may participate.

Programmable

Lightning uses Bitcoin smart-contract primitives to coordinate conditional and routed payments.

Global

Payments may be transmitted across geographic and political boundaries through internet-connected nodes.

Interoperable

Multiple open-source Lightning implementations follow shared protocol specifications.

Self-Custodial

Users may operate wallets and nodes that retain direct control over their signing keys and channel funds.

Foundation

Payment Channels

Channel Opening

A channel begins when bitcoin is committed to a funding transaction on the Bitcoin blockchain.

The funding output is governed by a shared contract that allows the channel participants to update how the funds are allocated.

Balance Updates

Participants exchange signed commitment transactions representing the latest channel balance.

These updates do not ordinarily need to be published to the blockchain.

Cooperative Close

Channel participants may agree upon a final balance and cooperatively publish a closing transaction.

Cooperative closure generally uses less block space and confirms more efficiently than a unilateral close.

Unilateral Close

Either participant may publish a valid commitment transaction if the other party becomes unavailable or refuses to cooperate.

Timelocks and revocation conditions help protect the most recent agreed channel state.

Network Routing

Routed Payments

A Lightning user does not need to open a direct channel with every person or service they wish to pay.

A payment may pass through a path of connected Lightning nodes. Each routing node forwards value from one channel to another.

The payment is constructed so that the route completes conditionally. Intermediate nodes do not need to trust the sender or recipient to honor an informal promise.

Routing depends upon channel connectivity, available liquidity, fee policies, payment size, timelock settings, node reliability, and the sender's ability to discover a workable path.

Conditional Payments

Hashed Timelock Contracts

Hashed Timelock Contracts combine a cryptographic hashlock with a deadline.

A recipient may claim a payment by revealing a secret value whose hash matches the condition committed to by the sender.

If the required secret is not revealed before the deadline, the payment can expire and the locked value can return through the appropriate refund path.

In a routed payment, linked cryptographic conditions coordinate settlement across each hop so that the payment completes or unwinds safely.

Payment Requests

Lightning Invoices

BOLT 11 Invoices

A Lightning invoice is an encoded payment request containing information needed to attempt a payment.

It may include an amount, payment hash, destination information, description, expiration time, routing hints, and other payment metadata.

Expiration

Invoices normally expire after a defined period.

Expiration reduces the risk that an old payment request will be reused after its original purpose has passed.

Amountless Invoices

Some invoices omit a fixed amount and allow the sender to choose how much bitcoin to pay.

These can be useful for donations, tips, variable purchases, and flexible contributions.

One-Time Use

Ordinary invoices are generally intended for a single successful payment.

Reusable payment experiences may rely upon generated invoices, offers, Lightning addresses, or interactive payment systems.

Reusable Payment Requests

Lightning Offers

Lightning Offers, specified through BOLT 12, are designed to support reusable, flexible, and privacy-improved payment requests.

An offer may represent a product, service, donation destination, recurring payment, or request that can be used multiple times.

The payer and recipient can exchange additional payment information before final settlement rather than placing every detail into a static invoice.

Support depends upon the wallet, node implementation, service provider, and protocol features in use.

Human-Readable Payments

Lightning Addresses

A Lightning address resembles an email address and can provide a human-readable destination for Lightning payments.

The receiving domain provides payment information and generates an invoice when a compatible wallet requests one.

Lightning addresses can improve usability, but they introduce dependence upon domain infrastructure, web services, certificates, server availability, and the operator hosting the address.

A Lightning address is an application-layer payment identifier and should not be confused with an on-chain Bitcoin address or a native Lightning protocol address.

Routing Privacy

Onion Routing

Lightning uses layered onion routing so that each forwarding node receives only the routing information required for its part of a payment path.

An intermediate node generally learns which neighboring node sent the payment and which neighboring node should receive it next.

It does not ordinarily receive the complete route in plaintext.

Onion routing improves privacy, but Lightning is not automatically anonymous. Timing, amounts, channel relationships, network observation, wallet behavior, routing failures, and service-provider records may reveal information.

Channel Capacity

Lightning Liquidity

Outbound Liquidity

Outbound liquidity is the portion of channel capacity available for a node to send.

Opening a channel with local funds generally creates outbound capacity from the opening node.

Inbound Liquidity

Inbound liquidity is the portion of channel capacity available for a node to receive.

A node may need incoming channels, circular rebalancing, liquidity purchases, swaps, or other tools to receive larger payments reliably.

Channel Balance

A channel's total capacity remains fixed unless capacity is changed or the channel is closed, while the allocation between participants changes as payments move.

Liquidity Management

Node operators may rebalance channels, adjust fees, open new channels, close inefficient channels, splice capacity, or use submarine swaps to manage liquidity.

Routing Economics

Lightning Fees

Routing nodes may charge a base fee, a proportional fee, or both for forwarding a payment.

The sender's wallet evaluates possible routes and their combined fees before attempting payment.

A route with the lowest advertised fee is not always the most reliable route. Wallets may consider past failures, available capacity estimates, node behavior, topology, and route length.

On-chain fees also matter when opening, closing, splicing, sweeping, or recovering Lightning channels.

Network Participants

Lightning Nodes

Personal Nodes

A personal Lightning node may provide self-custodial wallet functions, private payment infrastructure, independent channel management, and reduced dependence upon custodial services.

Routing Nodes

Routing nodes maintain channels with other participants and forward payments according to their liquidity and fee policies.

Reliable routing requires uptime, balanced capacity, secure operation, monitoring, and careful channel selection.

Merchant Nodes

Merchants may operate Lightning infrastructure to create invoices, receive payments, verify settlement, manage refunds, and integrate sales systems.

Service Nodes

Exchanges, payment processors, wallet providers, liquidity services, marketplaces, and application platforms may operate high-capacity Lightning nodes.

Lightning Labs

LND

Node Operation

LND can connect to a Bitcoin backend, maintain Lightning channels, participate in payment routing, create invoices, send payments, and expose administrative interfaces.

APIs

LND provides programmatic interfaces that applications can use to create invoices, inspect channels, monitor payments, manage peers, estimate routes, and administer the node.

Macaroons

LND uses macaroons to authorize access to node functions.

Different macaroons may grant different permissions, allowing applications to receive narrowly scoped access.

TLS

LND uses TLS for encrypted and authenticated communication with administrative and application interfaces.

Certificates, credentials, and macaroons must be protected because they may provide powerful access to node functions.

Protect Administrative Credentials

Administrative macaroons, wallet passwords, seed phrases, channel backups, TLS credentials, and private keys must not be exposed through public repositories, web roots, logs, screenshots, support messages, or unsecured application code.

Base-Layer Connection

Bitcoin Backend

A Lightning node requires reliable access to Bitcoin network and blockchain information.

Depending upon the implementation, the node may connect to Bitcoin Core or another supported Bitcoin backend and chain-data source.

Running Bitcoin Core provides independent validation of blocks and transactions and reduces dependence upon third-party blockchain services.

The Lightning node must monitor the blockchain for channel openings, confirmations, cooperative closures, unilateral closures, revoked states, sweeps, and other transactions affecting channel funds.

Custody Models

Lightning Wallet Types

Custodial Wallets

A custodial Lightning wallet holds funds and operates payment infrastructure on behalf of the user.

Custodial wallets may provide simple onboarding, but the service provider may control the keys and impose withdrawal, identity, account, or jurisdictional restrictions.

Self-Custodial Wallets

A self-custodial wallet gives the user control of the signing keys required to move funds.

The user remains responsible for backups, software security, recovery, channels, fees, and operational continuity.

Embedded Nodes

Some wallets include Lightning node functionality directly within the application and automate some channel and liquidity management for the user.

Remote-Control Wallets

A mobile or desktop wallet may connect to a separate Lightning node operated by the user.

This model can combine convenient interfaces with independently operated infrastructure.

Recovery

Channel Backups

Lightning recovery differs from ordinary on-chain wallet recovery.

A seed phrase may recover private keys, but additional channel information can be required to safely recover funds committed to Lightning channels.

Implementations may provide static channel backups, emergency recovery procedures, database backups, or implementation-specific restoration tools.

Backups should be stored securely, updated according to the implementation's requirements, protected from unauthorized access, and tested through documented recovery procedures.

Blindly restoring an outdated live channel database can be dangerous because obsolete channel state may conflict with the current state known by counterparties.

Channel Monitoring

Watchtowers

A Lightning participant using penalty-based channels must be able to respond if a counterparty publishes a revoked channel state.

A compatible watchtower can monitor the blockchain on behalf of a user and broadcast a preauthorized response when it detects a relevant breach.

Watchtowers can improve resilience for mobile users, intermittently connected nodes, and systems unable to monitor the blockchain continuously.

A watchtower is not a substitute for correct backups, secure keys, maintained software, reliable node infrastructure, or a documented recovery plan.

On-Chain and Off-Chain Exchange

Submarine Swaps

Submarine swaps exchange value between on-chain Bitcoin and Lightning without requiring an ordinary custodial deposit account.

A swap may allow a user to obtain Lightning liquidity by paying on-chain or move Lightning value back to an on-chain output.

Cryptographic conditions coordinate the two sides so that the exchange either completes or provides a recovery path.

Swap users should consider service fees, mining fees, timeout conditions, privacy, liquidity, counterparty availability, and implementation security.

Payment Reliability

Multipath Payments

A payment may be divided into smaller parts and sent across multiple routes.

This can improve reliability when no single route has enough available liquidity for the entire amount.

Atomic multipath payment mechanisms coordinate the parts so the recipient receives the intended payment only when the required conditions are satisfied.

Multipath payments may reduce dependence upon one channel or route, but they can also increase routing complexity, fee calculation, timing considerations, and information exposure.

Channel Management

Channel Splicing

Channel splicing allows funds to be added to or removed from a Lightning channel through an on-chain transaction without permanently ending the channel relationship.

A splice-in adds additional on-chain funds and increases channel capacity.

A splice-out removes part of the channel value and sends it to an on-chain destination.

Splicing can improve wallet flexibility by making Lightning and on-chain balances function more like components of a unified Bitcoin wallet.

Availability and interoperability depend upon implementation and wallet support.

Privacy

Lightning Privacy

Lightning can provide privacy advantages because most channel balance updates are not published individually to the Bitcoin blockchain.

Intermediate routing nodes generally receive only the information required to forward a payment to the next hop.

These properties do not make Lightning automatically anonymous.

Custodial wallet records, public channel announcements, invoice reuse, Lightning addresses, timing, payment amounts, route probing, network surveillance, IP addresses, web requests, and application logs may reveal information.

Privacy depends upon wallet architecture, node operation, routing behavior, network configuration, service providers, address practices, and the user's broader operational security.

Network Privacy

Lightning over Tor

Lightning nodes may use Tor onion services to communicate without exposing an ordinary public IP address to peers.

Tor can help protect network location, simplify inbound connectivity behind some network configurations, and reduce dependence upon publicly reachable IPv4 infrastructure.

Tor introduces latency, operational dependencies, relay availability considerations, and configuration requirements.

Using Tor does not remove blockchain analysis, application logs, payment metadata, custodial records, endpoint compromise, or other sources of identity exposure.

Operational Risks

Lightning Risks

Key Loss

Lost seeds, passwords, signing keys, or recovery records may make funds difficult or impossible to recover.

Channel-State Failure

Incorrect restoration, database corruption, or lost channel information can complicate recovery and closure.

Force Closures

Unilateral channel closure may create on-chain fees, waiting periods, and multiple sweep transactions.

Liquidity Failure

A payment may fail when available routes do not have sufficient usable capacity.

Software Defects

Wallets, nodes, plugins, APIs, integrations, and channel-management tools may contain implementation errors.

Custodial Risk

Custodial providers may freeze accounts, lose funds, suffer compromise, become insolvent, or discontinue service.

Fee Spikes

Rising Bitcoin mining fees can increase the cost of channel opening, closure, splicing, emergency recovery, and sweeping outputs.

Privacy Leakage

Routing activity, service records, invoices, IP addresses, and channel relationships can reveal sensitive information.

Responsible Operation

Node Security

Keep operating systems, Bitcoin software, Lightning software, plugins, dependencies, and wallet applications maintained and reviewed.

Restrict administrative interfaces, protect credentials, encrypt backups, separate public services from signing infrastructure, and avoid exposing privileged APIs directly to the public internet.

Use least-privilege access controls and separate credentials for monitoring, invoice creation, payment authorization, and administrative actions.

Monitor disk capacity, memory use, peer connectivity, Bitcoin synchronization, channel health, backup status, on-chain fee conditions, force closures, and pending transactions.

Test recovery procedures before committing substantial funds.

Never Share Wallet Secrets

Speciedex.org will never request a Lightning seed, Bitcoin seed phrase, wallet password, private key, macaroon, channel backup, authentication token, remote desktop session, or administrative node credential.

Independent Participation

Running a Lightning Node

Bitcoin Node

Operate or connect to a reliable Bitcoin backend that can validate or reliably provide required chain information, detect channel transactions, and broadcast on-chain transactions.

Lightning Software

Select a maintained Lightning implementation that supports the required platform, wallet model, API, backup method, and operational features.

Persistent Storage

Use dependable storage, adequate free disk space, controlled shutdown procedures, and verified backups.

Network Access

Configure reliable internet connectivity, peer access, firewalls, DNS, Tor, or public addressing according to the intended node model.

Channel Strategy

Open channels according to expected sending, receiving, merchant, routing, and liquidity needs.

Monitoring

Monitor synchronization, peers, balances, pending channels, force closes, routing activity, fees, backups, and system health.

Software Development

Building Lightning Applications

Invoice Integration

Applications can create invoices, display payment requests, monitor settlement, record payment state, and release services after successful completion.

Access Control

Lightning payments can authorize temporary access to digital content, APIs, downloads, services, or application functions.

Webhooks and Streams

Applications may subscribe to invoice and payment events rather than repeatedly polling the node.

Idempotency

Payment systems should prevent duplicate order fulfillment and correctly handle retries, expired invoices, partial failures, and delayed notifications.

Test Environments

Developers should test integrations using regtest, signet where supported, isolated nodes, small-value channels, and controlled failure scenarios.

Verification

Applications should verify settled payment state through their own trusted node or authenticated infrastructure rather than trusting browser input or client-side claims.
