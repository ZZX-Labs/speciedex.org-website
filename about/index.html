About Speciedex

Building Open Biodiversity Infrastructure

Speciedex is an open-source project dedicated to creating the world's most comprehensive digital index of biodiversity, combining taxonomy, scientific literature, conservation data, geographic information, and open software into a freely accessible public resource.

Mission

Speciedex exists to organize, preserve, and freely distribute biodiversity knowledge using open standards, open software, transparent development, and long-term digital preservation.

Biological information is currently distributed across thousands of journals, museums, universities, governments, field guides, databases, research organizations, and independent collections. While each contributes important knowledge, there remains no single open infrastructure designed to preserve and connect these resources for generations.

Speciedex seeks to become that infrastructure.

Rather than replacing existing scientific resources, the project provides common standards, open software, interoperable data formats, stable identifiers, and publicly accessible tools that allow researchers and institutions to work together more effectively.

Vision

Our long-term objective is to build a permanent digital index of every known species on Earth while providing a platform capable of incorporating future discoveries, taxonomic revisions, genetic information, conservation assessments, ecological data, media archives, and scientific literature.

The project is intended to remain useful for decades rather than individual software release cycles. Every architectural decision emphasizes longevity, interoperability, reproducibility, and public accessibility.

Speciedex is designed to serve researchers, educators, conservation organizations, museums, universities, governments, software developers, and the general public equally.

Why Bitcoin?

Speciedex is Bitcoin-native in philosophy and infrastructure. Bitcoin demonstrated that open protocols, public verification, distributed operation, cryptographic integrity, and long-term resilience can support critical global systems without dependence on a single controlling entity.

Speciedex applies those principles to biodiversity information. The project does not place every species record directly on the Bitcoin blockchain. Instead, Bitcoin-related technologies may be used where they provide clear value, including release verification, timestamping, data integrity, funding, distributed coordination, and permanent public references.

Cryptographic Verification

Dataset releases, software packages, metadata, and archival snapshots can be published with checksums and digital signatures for independent verification.

Timestamped Records

Important releases and research records may be anchored to publicly verifiable timestamps, creating durable evidence of publication and integrity.

Open Infrastructure

Bitcoin provides a model for operating public infrastructure through open protocols, independent implementations, distributed participants, and transparent review.

Global Funding

Bitcoin and the Lightning Network can support international donations, grants, bounties, contributor payments, and project funding without unnecessary payment barriers.

Data and Scientific Standards

Speciedex is designed to preserve source attribution and distinguish verified scientific information from unreviewed submissions, historical names, disputed classifications, provisional records, and community observations.

Each species record may include accepted scientific names, synonyms, taxonomic hierarchy, authority citations, common names, geographic distribution, habitat, ecology, conservation status, images, audio, video, genetic references, specimen records, publications, and revision history.

Stable Identifiers

Records use stable internal identifiers so taxonomic revisions, name changes, and merged records do not break historical references.

Source Attribution

Imported and contributed data should retain its original source, license, publication, author, institution, and retrieval information.

Revision History

Changes should be documented through transparent revision records that identify what changed, when it changed, and why.

Validation States

Records may be marked as verified, reviewed, provisional, disputed, deprecated, historical, incomplete, or awaiting expert review.

Open Formats

Data exports should use documented, machine-readable formats that can be archived, inspected, converted, and reused without proprietary software.

Interoperability

The project aims to support established biodiversity standards and cross-reference external identifiers where licensing and technical compatibility permit.

Project Scope

Speciedex is intended to document known life across all recognized biological kingdoms and taxonomic ranks. It may include living species, extinct species, fossil taxa, microorganisms, disputed taxa, historical classifications, hybrids, subspecies, varieties, forms, and other scientifically useful classifications.

The project may also preserve related records that help explain biodiversity, including ecosystems, habitats, biogeographic regions, specimens, genetic sequences, field observations, conservation assessments, ecological relationships, and scientific publications.

Inclusion within Speciedex does not automatically imply scientific acceptance. The database is designed to record uncertainty, disagreement, competing classifications, and changes in scientific consensus rather than conceal them.

Governance

Transparent development, documented decisions, and independent review.

Speciedex is developed as an open-source project. Technical decisions, software changes, data-model revisions, release planning, and scientific standards should be documented publicly whenever practical.

The project is intended to support participation by independent developers, researchers, institutions, contributors, and infrastructure operators. No single participant should be required for the continued operation, verification, or preservation of the public system.

Public Development

Source code, issue tracking, technical proposals, release notes, and contribution procedures should remain publicly accessible.

Documented Decisions

Material architectural, scientific, and policy decisions should include a written rationale and a reviewable history.

Independent Review

Software, datasets, standards, and scientific records should be open to inspection and verification by qualified third parties.

Responsible Stewardship

Maintainers are responsible for protecting project integrity, preserving attribution, documenting uncertainty, and avoiding undisclosed conflicts of interest.

Development Model

Speciedex follows a modular development model. Each component may evolve independently while remaining compatible through documented interfaces, schemas, release formats, and validation rules.

Stable releases are intended for general use. Testing releases provide early access to new capabilities. Development builds may contain incomplete or experimental features and should not be treated as authoritative production systems.

Stable

Reviewed releases intended for production use, archival deployment, institutional adoption, and long-term support.

Testing

Preview releases used to evaluate new functionality, migration procedures, schemas, interfaces, and performance changes.

Development

Current source code containing active work, experimental features, unresolved issues, and changes that may not remain backward compatible.

Archived

Historical releases, datasets, documentation, checksums, and signatures preserved for reproducibility and long-term reference.

Preservation and Resilience

Biodiversity records may outlive the institutions, software platforms, and funding programs that originally created them. Speciedex therefore treats preservation as a primary technical requirement rather than a secondary archival task.

Public releases should be reproducible, independently downloadable, and suitable for storage by universities, museums, libraries, governments, research groups, community operators, and individual archivists.

Portable Archives

Public data snapshots should be available in documented formats that can be copied, inspected, restored, and migrated without proprietary services.

Multiple Mirrors

Independent mirrors reduce reliance on one host, provider, network, jurisdiction, or administrative organization.

Integrity Metadata

Checksums, manifests, signatures, release identifiers, and timestamps help verify archived files and detect corruption or unauthorized modification.

Backward Compatibility

Migration tools, documented schemas, versioned APIs, and historical mappings help preserve access as formats and software evolve.

Privacy and Sensitive Data

Open biodiversity data can create unintended risks. Exact locations of endangered species, nesting sites, breeding populations, rare specimens, protected habitats, and culturally sensitive ecological knowledge may require restricted or generalized publication.

Speciedex should not expose sensitive location data merely because it is technically available. Publication decisions should consider conservation risk, legal restrictions, indigenous data sovereignty, research ethics, contributor privacy, and the potential for exploitation.

Location Generalization

Sensitive geographic coordinates may be reduced in precision, delayed, restricted, or replaced with broader regional descriptions.

Contributor Privacy

Personal information should be collected only when necessary and handled according to published privacy and data-use policies.

Restricted Records

Some records may retain public metadata while withholding details that could endanger species, habitats, communities, or protected research sites.

Responsible Disclosure

Security, privacy, data-quality, and conservation-risk concerns should have documented reporting and review procedures.
