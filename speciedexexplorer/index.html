Interactive Biodiversity Discovery

SpeciedexExplorer

SpeciedexExplorer is the interactive search, mapping, comparison, visualization, and research interface for the Speciedex biodiversity platform.

It allows users to explore species, taxa, habitats, geographic regions, observations, specimens, conservation records, ecological relationships, scientific references, images, audio, video, and other linked biological data.

The interface is designed for researchers, students, educators, conservationists, naturalists, institutions, developers, field teams, and anyone seeking a structured way to explore life on Earth.

What SpeciedexExplorer Is

SpeciedexExplorer is the public discovery layer built upon SpeciedexCore.

It transforms structured biodiversity records into searchable pages, maps, tables, filters, taxonomic trees, relationship networks, timelines, galleries, comparisons, and downloadable research views.

The interface is intended to make complex scientific information easier to navigate without hiding source attribution, uncertainty, disagreement, or revision history.

Why an Explorer Is Needed

Biodiversity information is highly connected.

A single species may have several names, multiple geographic populations, different conservation assessments, hundreds of observations, many media records, changing classifications, and numerous ecological relationships.

SpeciedexExplorer provides a visual and interactive way to move through these connected records rather than requiring users to search separate databases independently.

Search

Unified Biodiversity Search

SpeciedexExplorer is designed to search across the linked entities maintained by SpeciedexCore.

A single search may return taxa, scientific names, common names, synonyms, places, habitats, observations, specimens, references, media, conservation assessments, organizations, and related records.

Results can be grouped by record type and refined using structured filters.

Search behavior should preserve alternate spellings, historical classifications, transliterations, diacritics, multilingual names, abbreviations, and source-specific identifiers.

Exact Search

Find records by scientific name, common name, identifier, specimen code, accession number, publication identifier, or exact phrase.

Expanded Search

Include synonyms, former combinations, alternate spellings, accepted names, historical names, and multilingual terms.

Field Search

Restrict queries to taxonomy, description, locality, habitat, authority, reference, identifier, or another structured field.

Ranked Results

Rank results according to exactness, accepted status, source authority, completeness, confidence, geography, and relevance.

Species Profiles

Interactive Species Pages

Each species page is designed as a structured portal into the records associated with a taxon.

A page may include accepted scientific name, authority, common names, synonyms, classification, descriptions, morphology, life history, behavior, ecology, habitat, range, conservation status, threats, population trend, media, observations, specimens, genetic records, ecological interactions, references, and revision history.

The interface can separate concise summaries from detailed technical sections so that general users and specialists can work from the same underlying record.

Source citations, confidence labels, disputed fields, and update dates should remain visible throughout the page.

Summary View

Presents key identification, range, habitat, conservation, and classification information.

Scientific View

Presents detailed records, authorities, synonyms, source assertions, identifiers, and revision history.

Geographic View

Presents known range, observations, protected areas, habitats, migration, elevation, and regional records.

Presents photographs, audio, video, illustrations, specimen images, maps, and licensed derivatives.

Tree of Life

Taxonomy Browser

The taxonomy browser allows users to move through biological classification as an expandable hierarchy.

Users may begin with a domain, kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, species, or another recognized grouping and inspect its descendants, parent lineages, accepted names, synonyms, and alternate classifications.

Source-specific taxonomies can be compared without presenting one authority as universally correct in every case.

Taxonomic changes such as splits, mergers, transfers, rank changes, and synonymization can be displayed as linked historical events.

Expandable Tree

Open and collapse branches while preserving the user's current taxonomic context.

Breadcrumb Lineage

Display the full path from the selected record to its broader classification.

Alternate Taxonomies

Switch between or compare classifications maintained by different authorities.

Change History

Review former names, former placements, splits, mergers, and revision sources.

Users may pan, zoom, filter, select features, inspect records, compare layers, and change the geographic scale of analysis.

Maps should distinguish observed occurrence from modeled range, historical range, seasonal range, introduced presence, uncertain presence, and locally extinct records.

Sensitive coordinates may be generalized, withheld, delayed, or replaced with broader geographic summaries.

Comparative Biology

Species and Taxon Comparison

SpeciedexExplorer can compare two or more taxa using a structured side-by-side view.

Comparisons may include classification, scientific names, common names, morphology, diagnostic characteristics, range, habitat, behavior, ecology, reproduction, diet, conservation status, threats, media, observations, genetic identifiers, and references.

Missing information should remain visibly different from confirmed absence.

Conflicting source values should be displayed with their source and effective date rather than reduced to an unexplained single value.

Taxonomic Comparison

Compare lineage, rank, synonyms, authorities, taxonomic opinions, and classification differences.

Trait Comparison

Compare size, anatomy, coloration, behavior, diet, reproduction, lifespan, and ecological role.

Geographic Comparison

Compare ranges, habitats, countries, elevations, observations, migration, and overlap.

Conservation Comparison

Compare status, population trend, threats, protections, assessments, and recovery actions.

Occurrence Evidence

Observation Explorer

The observation explorer allows users to inspect records documenting where and when organisms were observed.

Each observation may include date, time, location, coordinate uncertainty, observer, source platform, identification, review state, method, equipment, notes, media, and supporting evidence.

Users can filter observations by taxon, region, date, evidence type, source, identification state, observer, habitat, or confidence.

Observation counts should not automatically be interpreted as population counts because observation effort varies by place, time, accessibility, species visibility, and user activity.

Timeline View

Display observations chronologically to identify seasonal activity, historical records, and recent change.

Map View

Display permitted observations geographically with uncertainty and sensitive-data controls.

Evidence View

Review associated photographs, audio, video, specimens, notes, tracks, environmental DNA, or other evidence.

Identification History

Review proposed identifications, corrections, reviewers, disagreements, and final review state.

Conservation

Conservation Explorer

The conservation explorer connects species assessments, population trends, geographic scope, threats, legal protections, protected areas, recovery actions, and supporting references.

Users can search or filter species by conservation category, assessment authority, threat type, population trend, region, habitat, endemism, protection, or review date.

A species may have different statuses under global, national, regional, and local assessment systems.

SpeciedexExplorer should display the authority, date, geographic scope, criteria, confidence, and source for each assessment.

Threatened Species

Explore taxa categorized as vulnerable, endangered, critically endangered, threatened, or similarly classified.

Endemic Species

Identify taxa restricted to a country, island, mountain range, habitat, watershed, or other region.

Ecosystems

The habitat explorer allows users to begin with an environment and discover the organisms associated with it.

Habitat pages may describe climate, geography, vegetation, hydrology, substrate, depth, elevation, seasonality, ecological processes, human pressure, and conservation importance.

Species records can be connected to breeding habitat, feeding habitat, refuge habitat, migration habitat, seasonal habitat, nursery habitat, and other functional associations.

Habitat classifications may vary among sources and should remain attributable.

Terrestrial Habitats

Forests, grasslands, deserts, mountains, shrublands, tundra, caves, agricultural areas, and urban environments.

Freshwater Habitats

Rivers, lakes, ponds, wetlands, springs, floodplains, groundwater, and seasonal waters.

Ecological Networks

Relationship types may include predation, herbivory, pollination, seed dispersal, parasitism, disease, symbiosis, mutualism, commensalism, competition, decomposition, host association, nesting, and habitat engineering.

Each relationship should remain linked to its supporting observation, publication, geographic context, life stage, season, and confidence.

A relationship documented in one locality or life stage should not automatically be presented as universal across the species' entire range.

Natural-History Collections

Specimen Explorer

The specimen explorer connects biological collection records with institutions, collection events, identifications, images, publications, genetic accessions, and taxonomic history.

Users may search by institution code, collection, catalogue number, collector, locality, date, type status, preparation method, taxon, or associated publication.

The same specimen may have several historical identifications without losing its persistent collection identity.

Specimen availability, loans, physical location, and reproduction rights remain governed by the holding institution.

Scientific Evidence

Literature and Reference Explorer

The reference explorer connects taxa and biological assertions with journal articles, monographs, taxonomic descriptions, reports, datasets, museum catalogues, theses, archived websites, and other supporting sources.

Users can search by title, author, publication year, journal, publisher, identifier, taxon, geography, conservation topic, or linked record.

References can be grouped according to whether they support, revise, dispute, or historically document a particular claim.

Access to full text depends upon the publication license, repository, publisher, institution, or archive.

Place-Based Discovery

Regional Explorer

Regional pages allow users to begin with a country, territory, island, ecoregion, watershed, mountain range, protected area, ocean, sea, or custom geographic region.

A regional page may summarize native species, endemic species, introduced species, threatened taxa, observations, habitats, protected areas, research institutions, conservation programs, and major data sources.

Species richness and observation totals should be interpreted in relation to survey effort, geographic size, season, accessibility, and source coverage.

Regional boundaries may overlap and may be defined differently by political, ecological, hydrological, and scientific authorities.

Change Through Time

Timeline Explorer

Timeline views can present observations, taxonomic revisions, publications, conservation assessments, specimen collections, range changes, extinctions, rediscoveries, and project updates.

Users may examine a single species, taxonomic group, region, habitat, source, or conservation issue across a selected period.

Historical records should retain the names and classifications used at the time while linking to current interpretations.

Gaps in a timeline may reflect missing data rather than absence of the species or event.

Research Tools

A workspace can record the selected filters, query terms, source releases, date, software version, taxonomic authority, and export configuration used to generate a result.

Private workspaces may contain unpublished notes, restricted records, or draft analyses and should remain separate from public project pages.

Shared workspaces should clearly identify authorship, permissions, source versions, and whether the content has received scientific review.

Data Access

Export and Download Tools

SpeciedexExplorer may allow users to export selected public records in documented machine-readable formats.

Exports may include taxa, names, identifiers, observations, places, references, conservation records, ecological relationships, and media metadata.

Every export should preserve source identifiers, attribution, license information, retrieval date, Speciedex release, schema version, and relevant restrictions.

Sensitive locations, private submissions, non-redistributable media, embargoed records, and restricted data should not be included in ordinary public exports.

Programmatic Access

API-Powered Exploration

SpeciedexExplorer uses documented SpeciedexCore interfaces to retrieve records, run searches, load maps, resolve identifiers, compare taxa, and generate exports.

Separating the interface from the core API allows other websites, desktop tools, mobile applications, field systems, and institutional portals to use the same public data.

Client applications should not receive administrative credentials or unrestricted database access.

Public API use may be subject to rate limits, caching, attribution, licensing, and access policies.

Inclusive Design

Accessibility

SpeciedexExplorer is intended to remain usable with keyboards, screen readers, zoom, high-contrast settings, reduced motion, and alternative input devices.

Interactive maps, charts, galleries, and taxonomic trees should provide text alternatives and structured data views.

Color should not be the only method used to communicate conservation status, confidence, category, warning, or selected state.

Scientific names, abbreviations, icons, controls, and complex visualizations should include understandable labels and supporting descriptions.

Multi-Device Access

Responsive and Field-Ready Interface

SpeciedexExplorer is designed for desktop, tablet, mobile, large displays, and field-oriented devices.

Dense tables and complex comparisons should adapt to smaller screens through stacked records, expandable sections, horizontal data views, or simplified summary modes.

Map controls, filters, galleries, and search tools should remain usable through touch input without preventing keyboard operation.

Low-bandwidth modes may reduce media loading, simplify map layers, limit automatic requests, and prioritize text and cached records.

Disconnected Use

Offline Explorer

SpeciedexExplorer may support offline datasets and local search interfaces for field stations, schools, archives, research vessels, remote communities, and low-connectivity environments.

Offline packages may contain selected taxa, regional maps, taxonomic indexes, educational records, references, and licensed media.

Local observations and notes can be stored until a trusted connection becomes available.

Synchronization should preserve timestamps, identifiers, evidence, user edits, and conflict history.

User Experience

Personalization Without Data Lock-In

Users may choose preferred language, taxonomy, units, map layers, region, display density, accessibility settings, saved searches, and frequently used filters.

Personalization should remain exportable and should not prevent users from accessing the underlying public records through standard interfaces.

Anonymous or local preferences may be used where an account is unnecessary.

Private research notes, saved collections, and account settings should not be exposed publicly without explicit user action.

Global Access

Multilingual Exploration

SpeciedexExplorer is intended to support multilingual interfaces, common names, educational text, regional terminology, and search.

Scientific names remain available across languages, while common names may vary by country, region, community, language, and writing system.

Transliteration, diacritics, alternate spellings, and multiple scripts should be considered in search and display.

Translated summaries should identify their language, source, translator or process, review state, and update date where practical.

Responsible Data Access

Privacy and Sensitive Records

Some biodiversity records contain personal information, private localities, unpublished observations, institution-only notes, or sensitive conservation data.

SpeciedexExplorer should expose only the fields approved for the current user and context.

Public pages and exports should not reveal private observer details, restricted specimen locations, unpublished research, administrative notes, credentials, or exact locations of vulnerable species when disclosure creates credible risk.

Access to restricted records should be logged, limited, attributable, and revocable.

Defensive Interface Design

Explorer Security

SpeciedexExplorer is a public-facing application and must treat queries, uploads, URLs, filters, imported text, embedded media, and user input as untrusted.

The interface should use strict output encoding, validated parameters, controlled file handling, limited query complexity, secure sessions, least-privilege APIs, and protected administrative routes.

Public clients should never contain database passwords, storage credentials, signing keys, administrative tokens, backup keys, or unrestricted API secrets.

Security controls should prevent unauthorized record modification, private-data disclosure, malicious media execution, cross-site scripting, injection, request forgery, and abusive automated extraction.

Efficient Discovery

Performance and Scale

SpeciedexExplorer may search and display millions of linked records, but it should not attempt to load every matching record into the browser simultaneously.

Results should use pagination, cursor-based loading, filtering, summary records, cached queries, and progressively loaded detail.

Large maps should cluster observations, simplify geometry, limit visible layers, and retrieve data according to the current geographic view.

Media should load responsively through appropriate derivatives rather than forcing every user to download original files.

Scientific Transparency

Data Quality Indicators

SpeciedexExplorer should present indicators describing the quality, completeness, source, age, and review state of a record.

A highly complete record is not automatically correct, and a sparse record is not automatically unreliable.

Quality indicators may describe source authority, identification confidence, geographic precision, evidence type, citation coverage, review status, conflict, age, and completeness.

Users should be able to inspect the evidence and source history behind important claims.

Verified

Reviewed against supporting evidence or an approved authoritative source.

Provisional

Available for discovery but awaiting further review or stronger evidence.

Disputed

Subject to conflicting evidence, interpretation, taxonomy, or source opinion.

Incomplete

Missing expected fields, evidence, geography, references, or review.

Reproducible Discovery

Saved and Shareable Views

Search results, comparisons, maps, taxonomic branches, galleries, and research collections may be represented by stable links or saved configurations.

A shareable view should preserve the relevant filters, record identifiers, taxonomic authority, geographic extent, sort order, and visible layers.

Where exact reproducibility is required, the view should also identify the Speciedex release or data snapshot used.

Private notes and restricted records must not become public merely because a user shares an otherwise public search or map.

Learning

Educational Exploration

SpeciedexExplorer can provide guided experiences for students, educators, families, museums, libraries, field courses, and independent learners.

Educational views may simplify technical presentation while preserving access to scientific names, sources, and deeper records.

Guided topics may include the tree of life, evolution, ecosystems, food webs, conservation, migration, adaptation, biodiversity hotspots, local wildlife, and scientific classification.

Educational summaries should remain clearly distinguished from primary records and specialist interpretations.

Community Participation

Citizen-Science Integration

SpeciedexExplorer may connect public observation records with taxonomic, geographic, conservation, and ecological information.

Users may review evidence, propose identifications, report incorrect metadata, add context, and identify relevant sources through moderated workflows.

Community agreement is useful but does not replace specialist review for difficult, cryptic, rare, or consequential identifications.

Sensitive records should receive appropriate geographic and privacy protection before public display.

Assisted Discovery

AI-Assisted Exploration

SpeciedexExplorer may use machine learning to assist with image search, acoustic matching, metadata extraction, record recommendations, duplicate detection, translation, taxonomic reconciliation, and anomaly review.

AI-generated suggestions should display model version, confidence, input type, review state, and relevant limitations.

Machine output should not be presented as a final scientific determination when evidence is uncertain.

Users should remain able to inspect the source records, evidence, and human review behind an accepted result.

Scientific and Technical Notice

SpeciedexExplorer is an evolving research, education, and software interface.

Biological classification, scientific names, geographic ranges, observations, conservation assessments, ecological interpretations, and source records may change.

Information may be incomplete, provisional, disputed, machine-assisted, outdated, source-limited, or awaiting review.

Maps may contain generalized, modeled, uncertain, historical, or restricted data and should not automatically be treated as exact real-time occurrence.

Users should verify important information through current primary literature, recognized scientific authorities, source databases, qualified specialists, and official agencies.

SpeciedexExplorer should not be used as the sole basis for medical, veterinary, legal, regulatory, wildlife-handling, trade, quarantine, navigation, enforcement, emergency, or public-safety decisions.
