Project History

The History of Speciedex

Speciedex began around 2013 as a theoretical concept: an open, durable, and extensible system for organizing, preserving, verifying, and exploring information about species and the natural world.

View the Timeline About Speciedex

Origins

From an Idea to a Long-Term Project

The earliest form of Speciedex was not a finished application or public service. It was a theoretical framework developed around 2013 for thinking about how biological, ecological, geographic, historical, and observational information could be represented within one coherent system.

The concept centered on creating more than a conventional species directory. The aim was to develop an adaptable technical foundation capable of connecting structured records, source material, classifications, observations, media, geographic context, and independently verifiable data.

Over time, this theoretical foundation developed into a broader research and engineering effort. Ideas were reviewed, reorganized, tested, discarded, restored, and expanded as available technologies and project requirements evolved.

Founding Principle

Preserve the record. Improve the system.

Speciedex is intended to remain useful across changing technologies, classifications, data sources, and scientific understanding.

Development Timeline

The following timeline provides a general history of the project. Dates and descriptions are intentionally broad placeholders and may be expanded as archival records, prototypes, documentation, and releases are formally cataloged.

  1. 2013

    Theoretical Origin

    The Initial Speciedex Concept

    Speciedex begins as a theoretical concept exploring how a comprehensive species index could combine classification, research, observation, geographic information, and historical records within a unified system.

  2. 2014

    Early Research

    Defining the Information Model

    Early research focuses on identifying the types of information a species record should contain and how records might connect to taxonomic, ecological, geographic, and documentary sources.

  3. 2015

    Structural Design

    Organizing Records and Relationships

    The concept expands beyond isolated entries toward linked records capable of representing relationships among species, environments, classifications, sources, and observations.

  4. 2016

    Technical Evaluation

    Reviewing Possible Architectures

    Different storage, indexing, search, and publication models are evaluated. Emphasis is placed on portability, long-term data preservation, traceability, and avoiding dependence on any single platform.

  5. 2017

    Prototype Planning

    Moving Toward Implementable Systems

    The project begins translating theoretical requirements into practical components, including record schemas, indexing methods, search tools, interfaces, and documentation structures.

  6. 2018

    Data Architecture

    Developing a Modular Record System

    Work continues on modular data structures designed to accommodate changing scientific classifications, multiple naming systems, uncertain records, revisions, and supporting evidence.

  7. 2019

    Verification

    Source Tracking and Data Provenance

    The project places greater emphasis on identifying where information originated, how records changed, and how users could distinguish sourced material from estimates, interpretations, or unverified submissions.

  8. 2020

    Distributed Access

    Resilience and Independent Operation

    Research expands into distributed access, mirrored data, offline use, local archives, interoperable services, and methods for maintaining access when centralized systems are unavailable.

  9. 2021

    Ecosystem Design

    Separating the Project into Components

    The architecture develops into a family of related components, allowing the public website, core software, exploration tools, network services, documentation, and data resources to evolve independently.

  10. 2022

    Consolidation

    Refining Scope and Terminology

    Earlier ideas are reviewed and consolidated. The project scope, terminology, technical boundaries, record concepts, and long-term objectives are reorganized into a clearer development framework.

  11. 2023

    Renewed Development

    Rebuilding the Public Foundation

    Development renews around a modern, accessible, standards-based public presence and a maintainable foundation for future software, data, documentation, and community participation.

  12. 2024

    Platform Definition

    Speciedex, Core, Explorer, and Network

    The project is organized around complementary platforms: Speciedex as the public project, SpeciedexCore as the underlying software, SpeciedexExplorer as the discovery interface, and SpeciedexNet as the supporting network.

  13. 2025

    Public Infrastructure

    Documentation and Website Development

    Public-facing infrastructure is expanded to support project documentation, software information, technical papers, resources, updates, releases, contribution guidance, and future data services.

  14. 2026

    Current Development

    Building the Speciedex Ecosystem

    Speciedex continues as an active research, software, infrastructure, and documentation project. Current work focuses on establishing a stable public foundation from which the larger ecosystem can be implemented, reviewed, tested, and expanded.

Project Evolution

What Changed Over Time

Then

A Theoretical Index

The earliest concept focused on creating a comprehensive and interconnected index of species-related information.

Development

A Technical Framework

The project gradually became a broader framework for structured records, provenance, search, interoperability, preservation, and distributed access.

Now

An Open Ecosystem

Speciedex is being developed as an ecosystem of public resources, software, documentation, network services, and exploration tools.

Continuity

A Project Designed for Revision

Scientific records change. Species are reclassified. Names are revised. New evidence appears. Old records are corrected. Technologies become obsolete.

Speciedex is therefore designed around revision rather than finality. Its history is not presented as a straight path from idea to finished product, but as an ongoing process of research, restructuring, implementation, and review.

The project history will become more detailed as original notes, prototypes, development records, release information, and archived materials are prepared for publication.
